📊 Bench reps at % of 1RM — research data

Strength fade — use it or lose it

How a lift's estimated 1RM fades when you stop training it (the “Current strength” setting). Full strength for two weeks, then about 10% gone a month later, slowing down after that and never dropping below half. Drag to pan · wheel to zoom.

How the numbers work

1. Effective load. For lifts that move your body (pull-ups, squats…), the load is coeff × bodyweight + added weight. coeff 0 = bar only (e.g. bench); 0.95 ≈ a strict pull-up. Bench at coeff 0 just uses the weight on the bar.

2. Epley 1RM (estimated one-rep max): load × (1 + reps / 30). A single rep returns the load itself. Heavier-for-more-reps ⇒ higher estimate.

3. Brzycki 1RM (alternative): load × 36 / (37 − reps). Undefined at 37+ reps, so it's blank there.

4. Nuzzo 1RM (bench curve). Instead of one fixed formula, this reads a research curve built from real bench data: your reps tell us what % of 1RM the load was, then 1RM = load ÷ %. On bench people do fewer reps per %1RM, so it gives a more conservative max than Epley. Pick it as the dashboard-wide formula in ⚙ Settings.

5. Volume: weight × reps — total load moved in the set.

6. Per bodyweight (the relative leaderboard): 1RM ÷ bodyweight, shown as ×BW, so people of different sizes compare fairly.

Merged exercises

Different spellings of the same lift are combined so one exercise isn't split across the dashboard. Combining two genuinely different names (e.g. Chin Ups → Pull Ups) is only ever done when confirmed as the same lift. Every combine is listed here; the original logged name is kept on each set.

Bodyweight parts

Share of bodyweight each movement lifts. The 1RM uses part × bodyweight + added weight. Unlisted exercises are 0. Choose how to group below; click a group to open or close it. When the app-wide filter is on, lifts it hides are greyed under “Show hidden”.

▸Case note — Maria (neck injury → handstands)
  • Headstands → neck injury
  • Switched to handstands
  • Handstands with wall assistance (method to confirm)

Health & safety — things to think about

  • High blood pressure. This matters here because handstands and headstands are inversions that push blood pressure up further. Get a professional's OK to invert, keep holds short, never hold the breath or strain (no Valsalva), and come down slowly — stop if the head pounds, ears ring, or vision swims. (confirm cleared to invert)
  • Neck still hurts — the original injury site. Keep load off the neck: this is exactly why we moved to handstands, not headstands.

Where she is now — numbers to beat

  • Dead hang: 27 s
  • Finger curls: 20 kg × 20 reps
  • Push-ups: pretty strong — count unknown (get a max-rep number). (confirm)
  • Box headstands: can barely lower the legs — the slow lowering (eccentric) is the current limit. Note: this is a headstand, so mind the neck + the blood-pressure caution above.
  • Back-to-wall handstand — current level: ladder + box at 15 cm from the wall, doing shoulder taps to the box for ~10 reps (this is the Ladder rung of her grid below).

What she wants to train

  • Grip
  • Glute medius (hip abduction — side-lying, cable, or banded)
  • Pull-ups

Open question

  • Does she want to keep her muscles smaller? Owner thinks not, but it's unconfirmed — settle this before pushing for size. (confirm)

Weekly plan — one session (everything in one day)

  • Frequency: ~once a week, sometimes once per 2 weeks — so one full session has to cover it all.
  • Goals: hip hinge · handstands · keep lower back + adductors healthy · improve pull-ups (if time) · grip + traps · glute medius.
  • Grip & traps: trained through the handstands — loading the tight traps to release them, and the wrists carry the grip. No separate work needed.
  • Adductor issue: nature to confirm (confirm).
  • Every set: external cues only · stop at neutral, no rounding · film + score.

The session — do it all

  • Warm-up: dowel hinge + wall hinge
  • Handstands: back-to-wall grid + lift shoulders off box (covers traps + wrists / grip)
  • Hinge: heavy floor deadlift she can't cheat (anchor), then Single-Leg RDL / RDL with dowel — slow + pause
  • Lower back: Roman Chair Side Bend
  • Adductors: Hip Adduction (full range) + Cable Hip Adduction
  • Glute medius: Hip Abduction (side-lying / cable / banded) — the abduction counterpart to the adductor work
  • Core slow: Lying Leg Raise, Decline Sit-Up (controlled tempo)
  • If time: Pull-ups (working goal)

Session notes & coaching cues (latest)

  • Always prepare supersets for her. Have the superset pairings set up before the session.
  • Heavy rows — form breaks down: moves too fast, form goes bad. Slow it down / drop the weight to hold form (same fast-momentum habit as the hinge).
  • Row hold: the isometric hold looks ok.
  • Adductor machine: 20 kg is a bit too much as a warm-up weight — start lighter.

Hip hinge — stop the rounding

  • Pattern: rounds the back unless the load is heavy; fast / momentum-driven; struggles with slow control.
  • Plan: keep the heavy "can't-cheat" pulls, add self-correcting constraints + external cues, cap range to neutral.
