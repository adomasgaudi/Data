How the numbers work

1. Effective load. For lifts that move your body (pull-ups, squats…), the load is coeff × bodyweight + added weight. coeff 0 = bar only (e.g. bench); 0.95 ≈ a strict pull-up. Bench at coeff 0 just uses the weight on the bar.

2. Epley 1RM (estimated one-rep max): load × (1 + reps / 30). A single rep returns the load itself. Heavier-for-more-reps ⇒ higher estimate.

3. Brzycki 1RM (alternative): load × 36 / (37 − reps). Undefined at 37+ reps, so it's blank there.

4. Nuzzo 1RM (bench curve). Instead of one fixed formula, this reads a research curve built from real bench data: your reps tell us what % of 1RM the load was, then 1RM = load ÷ %. On bench people do fewer reps per %1RM, so it gives a more conservative max than Epley. Pick it as the dashboard-wide formula in ⚙ Settings.

5. Volume: weight × reps — total load moved in the set.

6. Per bodyweight (the relative leaderboard): 1RM ÷ bodyweight, shown as ×BW, so people of different sizes compare fairly.

Bodyweight parts

Share of bodyweight each movement lifts. The 1RM uses part × bodyweight + added weight. Unlisted exercises are 0.

How to get stronger — the short version

You don't need a complicated program. Strength comes from a few simple things done consistently for months. Here's the whole picture, plain and honest.

1. Progressive overload — the one rule that matters

Muscles get stronger when you ask them to do a little more than last time: one more rep, a touch more weight, one more set, or cleaner technique. If the work never gets harder, you stop adapting. Each week, try to beat one number from the week before. Log your sets here so you can actually see it happening on the Athlete → Progress graph.

2. Pick a weight by how many reps it allows

Most growth and strength happens training in the 5–12 rep range, stopping 1–3 reps short of failure (you should believe you had a couple more in the tank). Heavier (≈1–5 reps) builds raw strength; lighter (12–20) builds endurance and is easier on the joints. As a rough guide, ~80% of your 1RM is about 8 reps, ~70% about 14. The Test tab turns any weight × reps into an estimated 1RM, and the bench reps-at-%1RM page shows the real research behind those numbers.

3. Hit each muscle about twice a week

Training a muscle 2× per week beats once a week for most people — more chances to practise and grow. A simple split: upper/lower twice each, or full-body three times. Aim for roughly 10–20 hard sets per muscle per week total, built up gradually.

4. Technique and full range of motion first

A clean rep through a full range of motion builds more muscle and protects your joints. Don't trade form for a bigger number — a heavier weight you half-rep is worse than a lighter one you control. Master the movement, then add load.

5. Rest enough between sets

For heavy compound lifts, rest 2–3 minutes between sets so the next set is actually hard for the muscle, not your lungs. For smaller isolation work, 60–90 seconds is plenty. Rushing your rest just lightens the effective load.

6. Recovery is when you grow

Training is the stimulus; sleep, food and rest are the adaptation. Get 7–9 hours of sleep, eat enough total food, and aim for roughly 1.6–2.2 g of protein per kg of bodyweight daily. Take at least a day or two off heavy lifting each week. If a lift stalls for weeks, you're often under-recovered, not under-trained.

7. Warm up briefly, then work

A few minutes of light movement plus 2–3 ramp-up sets on your first big lift is enough. You don't need to exhaust yourself warming up — just wake the pattern up and protect the joint.

8. Be patient and consistent

Strength is a multi-year project. Months of showing up and adding a little beat any perfect program done for three weeks. Track it, trust the trend, and keep going.

This is general education, not medical or coaching advice. If you have an injury or condition, check with a professional before starting.
